Title            : US Actions, Oil Market Volatility, and Global Consequences in the Iran-Israel Conflict (April–November 2024)
Date             : Current
Audience         : Analysts and Policymakers

1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Since April 2024, the United States has executed a coordinated military, diplomatic, and economic strategy to manage the escalating Iran-Israel conflict. Militarily, the US directly intercepted Iranian attacks and reinforced its regional defense posture with carrier strike groups and a THAAD battery. Diplomatically and economically, Washington imposed sweeping sanctions targeting Iranian military production and oil exports while simultaneously mediating ceasefire proposals for Gaza and Lebanon. These geopolitical escalations drove significant volatility in the energy markets, with WTI crude oil experiencing event-driven spikes of up to 5.15% following explicit strike threats. Globally, the conflict has caused a 50% drop in Suez Canal maritime trade, stalled major infrastructure projects like the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC), and accelerated a diplomatic decoupling between the West and the Global South.

2. INTRODUCTION
Following the April 2024 escalation between Israel and Iran, the regional conflict transitioned into unprecedented direct military engagements. The United States has played a central role in attempting to deter a wider regional war while supporting Israeli defense. This report synthesizes official US military deployments, diplomatic negotiations, and economic sanctions from April to November 2024. It further assesses the conflict's immediate macroeconomic impacts, focusing on WTI crude oil price movements and the broader global consequences for trade and international alliances.

3. METHODOLOGY NOTE
Findings in this report are synthesized from official statements from the US Department of Defense, US Central Command, US Department of the Treasury, and US Department of State. Economic and trade data relies on the US Energy Information Administration (EIA), primary financial news agencies (Reuters, Bloomberg, CNBC), and international bodies such as UNCTAD. Operational security parameters mean exact locations of certain stealth assets and classified back-channel communications are excluded from this analysis. 

4. FINDINGS

4.1 US Military Interventions and Posture
The US military has engaged in both direct defensive actions and strategic deployments to deter Iranian escalation. On April 13–14, 2024, US forces, including destroyers and F-15E aircraft, intercepted over 80 unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) and at least six ballistic missiles targeting Israel (Source: US Central Command, *US Central Command Update on Iran’s Attack on Israel*, 2024, https://www.centcom.mil/MEDIA/PRESS-RELEASES/Press-Release-View/Article/3740601/us-central-command-update-on-irans-attack-on-israel/). Similar actions occurred on October 1, 2024, when US Navy destroyers fired approximately a dozen interceptors to assist in defending against nearly 180 Iranian ballistic missiles (Source: US Department of Defense, *Pentagon Press Secretary Maj. Gen. Pat Ryder Holds a Press Briefing*, 2024, https://www.defense.gov/News/Transcripts/Transcript/Article/3922896/pentagon-press-secretary-maj-gen-pat-ryder-holds-a-press-briefing/). 

To bolster deterrence, the US escalated its regional presence. In August 2024, the USS Abraham Lincoln Carrier Strike Group and the USS Georgia submarine were deployed to establish a dual-carrier presence alongside the USS Theodore Roosevelt (Source: US Department of Defense, *Statement from Secretary of Defense Lloyd J. Austin III*, 2024, https://www.defense.gov/News/Releases/Release/Article/3869111/statement-from-secretary-of-defense-lloyd-j-austin-iii-on-us-forces-in-the-middl/). In October 2024, the US deployed a Terminal High Altitude Area Defense (THAAD) battery, along with approximately 100 US personnel, directly to Israel (Source: US Department of Defense, *Secretary of Defense Directs the Deployment of a THAAD Battery to Israel*, 2024). Further assets, including B-52 bombers, were ordered to the Middle East in November 2024 (Source: US Department of Defense, *Statement by Pentagon Press Secretary*, 2024).

4.2 US Diplomatic Initiatives and Economic Sanctions
The US implemented an extensive sanctions regime synchronized with active ceasefire negotiations. On April 18 and April 25, the Treasury sanctioned numerous individuals and entities enabling Iran's UAV and missile programs (Source: US Department of the Treasury, *Treasury Sanctions Iran’s UAV Program*, 2024). In June, sanctions targeted a "shadow banking" network moving billions for Iran's military (Source: US Department of the Treasury, *Treasury Sanctions Shadow Banking Network*, 2024). Following the October missile attacks, sanctions expanded to Iran’s petroleum sector and its "Ghost Fleet" of evasion tankers (Source: US Department of the Treasury, *Treasury Expands Sanctions on Iran’s Petroleum and Petrochemical Sectors*, 2024).

Diplomatically, the US advanced a three-phase Gaza ceasefire proposal on May 31, successfully securing UN Security Council Resolution 2735 in its support (Source: The White House, *Remarks by President Biden on the Middle East*, 2024; UN Security Council, *Resolution 2735*, 2024). The US later mediated high-level talks in Doha in August and pushed a 21-day Lebanon ceasefire proposal in September (Source: US Department of State, *Joint Statement from the United States, Egypt, and Qatar*, 2024; The White House, *Joint Statement on Lebanon*, 2024).

4.3 WTI Crude Oil Price Movements
Global energy markets reacted directly to escalatory geopolitical events. Between April 1 and 12, WTI prices rose 4.5% to $86.91 in anticipation of an Iranian strike (Source: U.S. Energy Information Administration, *WTI Spot Price Data*, 2024). Upon the highly choreographed April 13 strike, prices actually dipped to $85.41 (Source: CNBC, *Oil prices fall after Iran's attack on Israel*, 2024). However, sudden escalations caused immediate jumps: the July 31 assassination of Ismail Haniyeh triggered a 4% spike (Source: Bloomberg, *Oil Jumps Most Since October*, 2024), and the October 1 missile attack caused a 2.4% rise (Source: Reuters, *Oil prices jump 2%*, 2024). The highest geopolitical risk premium manifested on October 3, when WTI surged 5.15% after President Biden noted discussions regarding potential Israeli strikes on Iranian oil facilities (Source: CNBC, *Oil jumps 5% as Biden says US is discussing possible Israeli strike*, 2024).

4.4 Global Consequences and Trade Disruptions
US and allied actions succeeded in creating a unified Western sanctions front but faced severe global headwinds. Maritime trade via the Suez Canal dropped by 50% between April and August 2024 due to conflict in the Red Sea, forcing reroutes around the Cape of Good Hope that added 10-15 days to global shipping times (Source: UNCTAD, *Navigating Troubled Waters*, 2024). Furthermore, the India-Middle East-Europe Economic Corridor (IMEC) has stalled indefinitely due to the collapse of Israel-Saudi normalization prospects (Source: Lowy Institute, *IMEC: The economic corridor caught in the crossfire*, 2024). The conflict has also driven diplomatic decoupling; major Global South economies increasingly view US military support for Israel as contradictory to international law norms, increasing the geopolitical appeal of the BRICS+ bloc (Source: International Crisis Group, *The Global South and the Middle East Crisis*, 2024).

5. DISCUSSION
The findings reveal a dual-track US strategy: utilizing decisive military force and economic sanctions to contain Iran, while aggressively pursuing diplomatic ceasefires in Gaza and Lebanon. However, the evidence suggests limitations to this containment. While the US successfully defended Israel from mass-casualty missile events and managed to prevent an immediate regional war, it has been unable to secure maritime chokepoints in the Red Sea or maintain the progress of strategic trade corridors (IMEC). Furthermore, the heavy reliance on sanctions and military force has generated diplomatic friction with the Global South.

6. CONCLUSION
The United States has actively managed the Iran-Israel conflict since April 2024 through robust military deployments, comprehensive economic sanctions, and intensive diplomatic mediation. While these actions successfully thwarted direct Iranian attacks and stabilized immediate energy markets, the enduring consequences—a 50% reduction in Red Sea shipping, a frozen Middle Eastern economic corridor, and growing estrangement from the Global South—indicate that the broader regional crisis remains uncontained.
