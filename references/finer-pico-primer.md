# FINER & PICO(T) Primer

A quick reference for coaching users through research question refinement.

---

## FINER Criteria

Use these five tests to evaluate whether a research question is worth pursuing.

| Letter | Criterion       | Key question to ask the user                                                                                     |
| ------ | --------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| F      | **Feasible**    | Can this be answered with publicly available sources, within a reasonable time frame, without specialist access? |
| I      | **Interesting** | Does the question have a clear "so what?" — would the answer matter to someone?                                  |
| N      | **Novel**       | Does this ask for new synthesis or analysis, rather than a simple lookup of known facts?                         |
| E      | **Ethical**     | Does this question require handling sensitive data, vulnerable populations, or potentially harmful information?  |
| R      | **Relevant**    | Who benefits from the answer, and in what practical way?                                                         |

### Failure patterns and how to fix them

| Failure             | Example                                                       | Fix                                                                           |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Too broad           | "What is AI?"                                                 | Narrow to a specific application, population, or outcome                      |
| Not novel           | "What is the capital of France?"                              | Check if the answer is already universally known — if so, reframe as analysis |
| Not feasible        | "What are classified government AI projects?"                 | Redirect to publicly available policy documents or academic literature        |
| Ethically sensitive | "How do I profile individuals using their social media data?" | Flag, document in brief, escalate to user for guidance                        |
| Irrelevant          | "Write a poem about machine learning"                         | Clarify — this is creative work, not research                                 |

## PICO(T) Framework

Use this to structure a clinical, policy, or comparative research question.

| Letter | Element                     | What it covers                     | Example                                               |
| ------ | --------------------------- | ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| P      | **Population / Problem**    | Who or what is being studied?      | Adults over 40 with Type 2 diabetes in Southeast Asia |
| I      | **Intervention / Exposure** | What is being done or experienced? | Low-carbohydrate diet                                 |
| C      | **Comparator**              | What is it compared against?       | Standard dietary advice                               |
| O      | **Outcome**                 | What is being measured?            | HbA1c levels after 6 months                           |
| T      | **Time** (optional)         | Over what period?                  | 12-month follow-up                                    |

### When PICO(T) is not a clean fit

Not all research questions are comparative. For descriptive or exploratory questions,
use a simplified version:

- **Who** is being studied?
- **What** is being examined?
- **In what context?**
- **What outcome or finding is expected?**

This still gives the Research Lead enough structure to decompose the task.

## Common Refinement Prompts

Use these when a user's question needs work:

- "That sounds like a topic rather than a question — could you frame it as something
  that has a specific answer?"
- "Who would you say this research is really for, and what would they do with the
  answer?"
- "Is there a particular time period or geographic region that matters for this?"
- "When you say [vague term], do you mean [option A] or [option B]?"
- "Is this question asking what exists, or asking which option is better?"
