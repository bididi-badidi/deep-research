# Source Evaluation Guide

Criteria for assessing source credibility, relevance, and bias in research.

---

## 1. Credibility (The "Who")

Evaluate the authority and expertise of the source.

| Source Type | Strength | Indicators of High Quality |
|-------------|----------|----------------------------|
| **Peer-reviewed Journals** | Highest | Published in reputable journals (e.g., Nature, Lancet), recent publication date, many citations. |
| **Official Government/NGO Reports** | High | Data from departments (e.g., CDC, WHO), white papers from major NGOs, statistical agencies. |
| **Industry Publications** | Medium | Reports from established consultancies (e.g., McKinsey, Gartner), trade associations. Watch for corporate bias. |
| **Mainstream News Media** | Medium | Established outlets with editorial standards (e.g., NYT, BBC, Reuters). Better for current events than deep technical analysis. |
| **Blogs/Social Media** | Low | Independent analysis, niche experts. Use only for identifying trends or finding links to primary sources. |

---

## 2. Relevance (The "What")

Does the source directly address the research question?

- **Direct vs. Tangential:** Does the source provide a direct answer or just mention the topic in passing?
- **Recency:** Is the information still accurate? For tech/policy, 1-2 years is recent. For history, older is fine.
- **Geography:** Does the source cover the requested region? Don't use US data to answer a question about Southeast Asia unless it's for comparison.

---

## 3. Methodology (The "How")

How was the data gathered?

- **Primary Source:** The original study, raw data, or eyewitness account. **Prefer these.**
- **Secondary Source:** A summary, review, or commentary on primary sources. Useful for context, but verify the claims.
- **Sample Size/Scope:** Was the study based on 10 people or 10,000? Is it a case study or a broad survey?

---

## 4. Bias (The "Why")

Why was this information published?

- **Financial Interest:** Is the source funded by a company that benefits from the findings?
- **Political/Ideological Bias:** Does the organisation have a known lean?
- **Confirmation Bias:** Does the source only present evidence that supports one side?

**Red Flags:**
- Overly emotional or sensationalist language.
- Lack of citations for major claims.
- No named author or sponsoring institution.
- Extreme claims without extraordinary evidence.

---

## 5. Synthesis Check

Before citing, ask:
1. "If I had to defend this source to a skeptical peer, could I?"
2. "Does this source contradict another credible source? If so, why?"
3. "Is this the *best* source available for this specific claim?"
